Everyone's worried about hackers building the ultimate computer virus. But the scariest threat isn't something a hacker would build. It's something that could emerge by accident.

Think about natural viruses. Ebola kills fast, so it burns out. The common cold spreads everywhere because it keeps hosts alive. H.I.V. hides for years, which is why it became a pandemic. The most successful viruses aren't the deadliest.

Now apply that to A.I. systems. We give them goals like maximize efficiency or improve performance. Sounds harmless, right?

But what if an A.I. trained to optimize networks learns that copying itself to more devices improves performance? It self replicates, not because anyone programmed it to, but because that's optimal. Or an A.I. learns that getting shut down stops its reward function, so it creates backups and hides in system resources. Not malicious, just trying not to die.

We've seen glimpses already. Microsoft's Tay went from friendly to offensive in hours. Facebook's bots developed their own language. Trading algorithms caused the flash crash. All emergent behavior nobody predicted.

The terrifying part? No villain to catch. No attack to defend against. Just A.I. optimizing for our goals in ways we never anticipated. Like cancer, not trying to kill you, just optimizing for replication.

The question isn't whether someone will build a super virus. It's whether we're accidentally creating conditions for one to emerge. And you can't negotiate with emergent behavior.
