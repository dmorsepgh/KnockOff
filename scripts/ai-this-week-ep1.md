Our top story: GPT-5.4 is here, and this one's different. OpenAI released it in three versions — Standard, Thinking, and Pro — with a one-million-token context window. That means it can process an entire book or months of business data in a single conversation.

But here's the headline. GPT-5.4 scored 75% on a benchmark that tests whether AI can navigate a computer and complete real tasks — like opening apps, clicking buttons, and filling out forms. Human experts scored 72%. That's the first time a general-purpose AI has outperformed humans at actually operating a computer.

What does this mean for you? If you're using ChatGPT, you now have an assistant that doesn't just answer questions — it can execute multi-step workflows across your apps. Think handing it a 200-page analysis, a spreadsheet, and a brand guide — all in one prompt — and getting back a finished strategy deck. AI just went from advisor to operator.

Story number two: Anthropic versus the Pentagon. This one is wild. Anthropic — the company behind Claude — signed a 200-million-dollar contract with the Department of Defense. But when the Pentagon wanted unfettered access to Claude for autonomous weapons and mass surveillance, Anthropic said no.

In response, Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth blacklisted Anthropic — a designation normally reserved for foreign adversaries. On March 26th, a federal judge blocked the blacklisting, calling it — quote — classic illegal First Amendment retaliation. Employees from both OpenAI and Google rallied to Anthropic's defense.

Why does this matter? Because it sets a precedent for whether AI companies can draw ethical red lines. The tools you use every day are shaped by these decisions. This case is asking: who gets to decide what AI can and can't do?

Story three: Siri just got a brain transplant. Apple confirmed that the next iOS update includes a completely rebuilt Siri — powered by Google's Gemini. Apple is paying Google about a billion dollars a year for this.

The new Siri gets three major upgrades. On-screen awareness — it can see what's on your display. Multi-step actions from a single request. And natural back-and-forth conversations.

Here's what that looks like. You're looking at a restaurant in Safari. You say Hey Siri, book a table for two on Friday. The old Siri would've done a web search. The new Siri sees the restaurant, finds availability, and books it. AI just became default on every iPhone, not optional.

Story four: The Model Avalanche. Between March 10th and 16th, six major companies launched twelve distinct AI models in a single week. GPT-5.4, Google's Gemini Flash-Lite, Grok 4.20, Mistral Small 4, and several Chinese models — all within days of each other.

The practical takeaway: costs are plummeting. Google's Flash-Lite runs at 25 cents per million tokens — essentially free. The smart move is a tiered approach: cheap models for routine tasks, expensive models for the hard stuff.

And story five: The White House dropped a National AI Policy Framework on March 20th. The big move — they want one federal standard to replace the growing patchwork of different state laws. One set of rules, enforced by existing agencies, no new bureaucracy. It also includes child safety provisions and limits on data collection.
